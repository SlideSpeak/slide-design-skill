A 10-minute field note

How to read a balance sheet

A one-page walk-through of the statement that tells you what a company owns, what it owes, and what is actually left over.
Finance, sketched out
The one idea

A balance sheet always balances.

Everything the company owns was paid for somehow, either with borrowed money or with the owners' money. So what it owns must equal what it owes plus what is left over. That is the whole trick.
Remember
Assets = Liabilities + Equity. If it does not balance, something is wrong with the books, not the rule.
Label the parts

Three boxes, top to bottom

1
Assets
What the company owns. Cash, stock it can sell, buildings, machines. Listed most-liquid first.
2
Liabilities
What it owes to other people. Bills, loans, money due to suppliers. Sorted by when it is due.
3
Equity
What is left for the owners after the debts. Assets minus liabilities. The owners' real stake.
Do this in order

Four passes down the page

1
Find the cash
Start top-left. Cash is the line that cannot lie. A healthy firm has enough to cover the near-term bills.
2
Check the debts
Drop to liabilities. Split short-term from long-term. Lots due soon is a warning worth circling.
3
Read the equity
Look at what is left for the owners. Growing equity over years means the company is building value.
4
Test the balance
Add assets, add liabilities plus equity. They must match. Now you have read the whole statement.
The number to memorise
1.5
a comfortable current ratio
Divide what the company owns and can sell soon by what it owes soon. That is the current ratio. Around 1.5 means it can pay its near-term bills with room to spare.
So what
Below 1.0 means the bills due soon are bigger than the cash on hand. Circle it and ask why.

Before you trust it

A quick health check

Enough cash to cover the bills due in the next year
Debt that is shrinking, or at least not racing ahead of growth
Equity that has grown over the last few years
No single mystery line that is suspiciously large
The two sides actually add up to the same total
Tick all five and the statement is probably honest and healthy. Miss one and start asking questions.
Do not mix these up

Owns versus owes

An asset
A thing of value the company controls. It can be turned into cash, used to make money, or sold. It sits on the left.
A liability
A claim someone else has on the company. It is money that has to leave the business later. It sits on the right.
The takeaway: Same item, different side. A loan is cash you hold today and a debt you owe tomorrow.

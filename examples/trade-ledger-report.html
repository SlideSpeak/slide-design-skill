Global trade is rerouting faster than the tariffs that triggered it

Container flows, supplier maps and input costs are realigning around a smaller set of trusted corridors. The adjustment is structural, and it is moving quicker than most boards have planned for.
Three quarters into the realignment, the pattern is no longer noise. Trade between the largest blocs is contracting while a band of connector economies absorbs the rerouted volume. Freight rates, factory orders and inventory cover all point the same way: companies are paying a premium now to hold a supply chain they can predict later. This briefing reads the quarter through five questions, with the figures the desk is watching.

The premium for a predictable supply chain has become a line item, not a risk

What looked like a temporary detour around tariff walls in 2025 has hardened into a standing cost of doing business across three continents.
By the Trade Realignment Desk
Boards spent 2025 treating supply-chain disruption as weather: unpleasant, temporary, something to wait out. The figures this quarter say otherwise. The cost of rerouting around the largest trade blocs has stopped spiking and started flattening into a stable, higher baseline. That is the signature of a structural shift, not a shock.
The mechanism is straightforward. Firms are dual-sourcing inputs, holding more inventory, and paying to move goods through corridors that clear customs predictably rather than cheaply. Each choice is rational on its own. Together they have lifted landed input costs by roughly a tenth and they are not coming back down. The connector economies that sit between the blocs are the visible winners.
For planners the practical question has changed. It is no longer whether to diversify but how fast, and what to pay for resilience that competitors are already buying. The desk reads the quarter as the point where the realignment stopped being a scenario and became the operating environment.
The cheapest corridor and the most reliable corridor are no longer the same route, and balance sheets now show it.

In brief Q3 2027

Four dispatches from the quarter

Freight reroutes around two chokepoints
Vessels are taking longer trusted routes over shorter contested ones. Average voyage length on the Asia to Europe lane rose 9 percent, lifting rates even as total volume slipped.
Supplier maps shorten and thicken
The median manufacturer now lists 2.3 qualified suppliers per critical input, up from 1.4 a year ago. Redundancy is being bought deliberately, not discovered in a crisis.
Connector economies absorb the spillover
A band of mid-sized economies between the major blocs captured most of the rerouted assembly work, posting double-digit export growth while bloc-to-bloc trade contracted.
Inventory cover ratchets higher
Days of inventory on hand rose to 58 from 49 across surveyed sectors. Working capital is the price firms are paying to hold a chain they can predict.

The read
The curve is the whole argument. Costs climbed through 2025 and 2026 as firms rerouted and dual-sourced, then stopped rising in 2027. They are not retreating. An 18 percent higher baseline is the new floor, and planning that assumes a return to the old level is planning for a quarter that is not coming.
